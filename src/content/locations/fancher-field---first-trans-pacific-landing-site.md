# Fancher Field: Where the Pacific Was Conquered by Air

## The Tragic Beginning (1928)

The airfield that would witness one of aviation's greatest achievements began with tragedy. In April 1928, the site was just an informal landing strip in East Wenatchee, Douglas County, used for barnstorming shows and the occasional flight.

On **April 29, 1928**, Major John T. "Jack" Fancher, a World War One pilot and commander of Spokane's Air National Guard unit, came to perform in an air show demonstration. During the show, a bomb explosion fatally injured him. Within days, the field was renamed **Fancher Field** in his honor.

## Early Development (1920s-1930s)

Fancher Field faced unique legal complications in its early years. The City of Wenatchee wanted to develop the airfield, but state law prevented cities from owning land outside their county boundaries. This wasn't resolved until **1934**, when the law changed.

Throughout the 1930s, the **Works Progress Administration (WPA)** provided labor to improve the runways and facilities. The field remained relatively small and informal, rough, unimproved landing strips serving local pilots, barnstormers, and the occasional long-distance flier.

## October 5, 1931: The Day the Pacific Fell

At dawn on **October 4, 1931**, two American pilots took off from Sabishiro Beach near Misawa, Japan, in a red Bellanca monoplane called **"Miss Veedol"** (named after their sponsor, Veedol Motor Oil). Their goal: the **first non-stop trans-Pacific flight**.

**Clyde Pangborn** and **Hugh Herndon Jr.** knew the 5,500-mile journey would push them to the absolute limits of aviation technology. To save fuel and increase range, they made a radical decision: over the Pacific Ocean, they **jettisoned their landing gear**, planning to belly-land at their destination.

For **41 hours**, the two pilots flew through darkness, storms, and exhaustion. They had no weather forecasts, no radio beacons, and no way to land safely if something went wrong. Their only option was to keep flying east toward North America.

As dawn broke on **October 5, 1931**, they spotted the Columbia River and followed it to Wenatchee. Low on fuel and flying a plane with no landing gear, they circled Fancher Field and prepared for a belly landing.

At **7:12 AM**, Pangborn brought Miss Veedol down in a perfect skid across the dirt runway. The plane slid to a stop, intact. **The Pacific Ocean had been crossed by air for the first time in history**.

The flight covered **5,500 miles in 41 hours and 13 minutes**, an average speed of 133 mph. It shattered existing distance records and proved that trans-Pacific aviation was possible.

### The Heroes

**Clyde Pangborn** (1895-1958) was already an experienced stunt pilot and had performed as a wing-walker. After the trans-Pacific flight, he continued barnstorming and served as a test pilot during World War II.

**Hugh Herndon Jr.** (1904-1937) was an heir to a cattle fortune who financed much of the expedition. After their historic flight, the two pilots had a falling out over credit and money. Herndon died in a plane crash in 1937.

The two men won the **Harmon Trophy** for their achievement and received honors from the Japanese and American governments.

## The Monument

A **14-foot Pangborn-Herndon Monument** was erected at the landing site, commemorating the first trans-Pacific flight. It still stands today in the Fancher Heights subdivision, a permanent marker of the day two aviators changed aviation history.

## Civilian Operations (1930s-1960s)

After the famous 1931 landing, Fancher Field continued as a civilian airfield. It was never a major military installation during World War II, the federal government built **Pangborn Memorial Airport** nearby in 1941 for potential military use, though it was never actively used by the military.

In **1949**, the City of Wenatchee transferred Fancher Field to private operators. The field was used for:
- Pilot training
- Agricultural spraying operations  
- General aviation

Throughout the 1950s and 1960s, the field **gradually fell into disuse** as the region's aviation shifted to Pangborn Memorial Airport, which opened commercial service in 1945.

## The Subdivision (1960s-Present)

By the mid-1960s, Fancher Field had ceased operations. The runways were abandoned, and developers acquired the property.

Today, the historic airfield is the **Fancher Heights housing subdivision**. Streets like **Fancher Boulevard**, **Fancher Field Road**, and **Fancher Landing** preserve the name and memory of the aviation pioneer and the two pilots who crossed the Pacific.

The **Pangborn-Herndon Monument** remains at the original landing site, a granite reminder that this quiet residential neighborhood was once the endpoint of one of the most daring flights in aviation history.

## Legacy

Fancher Field represents the golden age of aviation, when pilots pushed the boundaries of what was possible with little more than courage, skill, and determination.

The first trans-Pacific flight opened the door to commercial air travel across the Pacific, connecting America and Asia in ways that would have been unimaginable just a generation earlier.

For those who lived in East Wenatchee in 1931, the sight of that red Bellanca sliding across the dirt runway must have seemed like witnessing the future arrive ahead of schedule.
