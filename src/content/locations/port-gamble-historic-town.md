# Port Gamble: The New England Town That Came West

## July 1853: Finding Paradise

Captain William Talbot stood on the deck of the schooner *Julius Pringle*, surveying the sheltered waters at the mouth of Gamble Bay. Behind him stretched Hood Canal. Before him lay dense forests of Douglas fir and western red cedar, timber so massive it would take crews days to fell a single tree.

The S'Klallam people called this place **"Teekalet"**, brightness of the noonday sun. Talbot saw something else: the perfect location for a lumber mill.

He had come from San Francisco, where the Gold Rush had created insatiable demand for building materials. Timber was worth more than gold. Every ship arriving in California needed lumber. Every prospector needed a house, a store, a saloon.

Talbot sent word to his partners back east: **Andrew Jackson Pope** and **Josiah Keller**, fellow merchants from East Machias, Maine. The message was simple: Come west. Bring workers. We're building a mill.

## Building a New England Village in the Wilderness (1853-1860)

In summer 1853, ten workers, most recruited from East Machias, arrived with lumber shipped around Cape Horn from Maine. They built the first structures: a bunkhouse, a cookhouse, and a general store.

In **September 1853**, the mill sawed its first log using a Muley saw powered by a steam engine. The **Puget Mill Company** was born.

But Pope and Talbot weren't just building a mill. They were building a **New England village** 3,000 miles from home.

### Combating Homesickness with Architecture

The founders understood that recruiting skilled workers to the frontier required more than wages. Workers needed to feel at home.

So they built Port Gamble to look exactly like East Machias, Maine:

- **White clapboard houses** with steep gabled roofs and picket fences
- **Elm trees** grown from Maine cuttings
- **A Gothic Revival church** using the same architectural plans as the church back home
- **Wide streets** laid out in a grid
- **Company-provided housing**, schools, a hospital, and social clubs

When workers stepped off the ship, they didn't see a rough frontier lumber camp. They saw home.

### The Thompson House (1859)

In 1859, **James A. Thompson** arrived on the schooner *Towana* after a grueling voyage around Cape Horn from Maine. He was a **saw filer**, one of the most skilled positions in a sawmill, responsible for sharpening and maintaining the massive circular saws that cut timber.

Thompson built a house on Rainier Avenue overlooking Gamble Bay. He built it in stages: first in 1859, then expanded in 1864 and again in 1872 as his family grew.

**The Thompson House still stands today**, the **oldest continuously occupied house in Washington State**. Thompson descendants lived there for 99 years. Three generations of Thompsons worked as saw filers at the Port Gamble mill: James (until his death in 1911), his son Will, and his grandson Robert.

The house has three chimneys, an 1860s fireplace, and the same wooden floors James Thompson walked on 165 years ago.

## The Company Town Model (1860s-1890s)

By the 1860s, Port Gamble had become a **classic company town**. Pope and Talbot owned everything:

- **All housing** (workers rented from the company)
- **The general store** (workers bought goods on credit against wages)
- **The hospital, school, and church**
- **The hotel and social clubs**

But unlike many company towns, Pope and Talbot treated workers relatively well. The philosophy was simple: **well-cared-for workers meant better profits**.

### What the Company Provided

**Housing**: Workers rented homes based on their position. Skilled workers like saw filers got larger houses. Mill laborers lived in dormitories.

**Medical care**: The company employed a physician and operated a hospital (1879-1929) in the New York House (built 1863).

**Education**: Port Gamble established the **first school in Kitsap County** in 1859.

**Recreation**: The company built a theater, sponsored dances and movies, and provided a community hall for basketball games and social events.

**Spiritual life**: **St. Paul's Episcopal Church** (1870/1879) was built using the same architectural plans as the church in East Machias, Maine. The Carpenter Gothic structure featured an original steeple and was fully restored by Pope & Talbot in 1973.

**The General Store**: The first store was built in 1853 from Maine lumber. The current building (the fifth iteration) dates to 1916. It served as the social and economic heart of the town, workers bought provisions, ship captains resupplied, and news was exchanged.

## The Golden Age (1870s-1920s)

By 1857, the Port Gamble mill was producing **8 million board feet annually**, the largest business on Puget Sound.

### Shipping Worldwide

The mill didn't just serve California. By 1862, Pope and Talbot owned **10 vessels** and shipped nearly **19 million board feet** to **37 ports worldwide**:

- **Shanghai** and other Chinese ports
- **Cape Town**, South Africa
- **British, French, and Russian navies**
- **Peru, England, Hawaii, Australia**

The company's ships returned from Asia loaded with tea, silk, and spices, creating a profitable triangular trade route.

### Victorian Architecture Boom

The 1880s-1890s were Port Gamble's architectural peak. Wealthy mill superintendents and company officers built grand Victorian homes:

**Walker-Ames House (1888-1889)**: A stunning Victorian Shingle Style mansion designed to welcome arriving ships. Two stories with attic and basement, it served as the home for mill superintendents. Today it's used as an event venue, and is reportedly the **most haunted building in Washington**.

**New York House (1863)**: Victorian-era building with chamfered porch pillars and jig-sawn brackets. Originally a company guesthouse, it later served as a physician's office and hospital. Now an antique shop called "The Painted Lady."

**Community Hall & Post Office (1907)**: Designed by Seattle architects Bebb & Mendel, this yellow building housed a post office (still operational today), community center, and second-floor auditorium for dances, movies, plays, and basketball.

### Peak Employment

By the **1920s**, the mill employed **250+ workers**. The town had everything a resident could need without ever leaving:

- General store
- Post office
- Hospital
- Schools
- Church
- Hotel
- Theater
- Social clubs
- Cemetery

Port Gamble was a self-contained world.

## Tragedy and Resilience: The 1856 Battle

Not all of Port Gamble's history is peaceful.

In **1856**, during the broader Indian Wars in Puget Sound, a party of **northern Natives** from Alaska, variously identified as Tlingit and Haida, had encamped near Port Gamble.

The USS *Massachusetts*, a Navy sloop-of-war, was anchored nearby. When the northern Natives refused demands to leave, the *Massachusetts* opened fire and shelled their encampment. In the fighting that followed, **Gustave Englebrecht**, a sailor aboard the *Massachusetts*, was killed, becoming the **first U.S. Navy casualty in Pacific waters**.

Englebrecht is buried in the **Buena Vista Cemetery** on the bluff above Port Gamble. His grave is marked and maintained to this day.

The cemetery also holds **Josiah Keller** (one of the mill's founders, who died in 1862) and generations of Port Gamble families. It has become part of the town's ghost tour circuit, Port Gamble is known as one of the most haunted towns in Washington.

## The Great Depression and World War II (1925-1945)

In **October 16, 1925**, the Puget Mill Company sold the Port Gamble mills to **McCormick Lumber Company** for **$15 million**. But the sale came just before the Great Depression.

In **1938**, during the Depression, Pope & Talbot **reacquired the property** via foreclosure. The company held on through the darkest economic years in American history.

During **World War II**, Port Gamble's lumber was vital to the war effort. The mill produced timber for:

- **Military construction** across the Pacific Theater
- **Shipbuilding** (wooden minesweepers and patrol boats)
- **Aircraft construction** (spruce for airplane frames)

The mill ran at full capacity throughout the war.

## The Long Decline (1950s-1995)

After World War II, the lumber industry began to change. Mechanization reduced the need for workers. Environmental regulations limited timber harvests. Cheaper lumber from the South and overseas undercut prices.

By the 1960s, Port Gamble's population was shrinking. Pope & Talbot began **restoring historic buildings** as a preservation effort and a hedge against the mill's uncertain future. The company:

- Relocated utilities underground
- Installed gas street lamps
- Restored Victorian homes and public buildings
- Designated the town a National Historic Landmark (1966)

But the economics of lumber had changed forever.

## November 30, 1995: The Last Log

On **November 30, 1995**, the Port Gamble mill shut down after **142 years of continuous operation**, the **longest-running sawmill in North America**.

The last log was sawed. The steam whistle, silent for the first time in living memory, echoed one final time across Gamble Bay.

In 1996, the mill machinery was auctioned off. Workers who had spent their entire lives in Port Gamble faced an uncertain future.

## Port Gamble Today: From Mill Town to Museum Town

After the mill closed, the question was simple: What happens to a company town when the company leaves?

Port Gamble's answer: **Become a living museum**.

### What Remains

**83 of 85 historic buildings** remain on their original sites. Most date from the 1860s to early 1900s. The town looks almost exactly as it did in 1890.

**Thompson House (1859)**: Washington's oldest continuously occupied home, still privately owned.

**St. Paul's Episcopal Church (1879)**: Fully restored, hosting services and weddings.

**General Store (1916)**: Ground-floor gift shop and café, second-floor Sea & Shore Museum (25,000+ seashell collection), basement Port Gamble Historic Museum.

**Community Hall & Post Office (1907)**: Fully operational post office, home to Port Gamble Theater.

**Walker-Ames House (1888-1889)**: Event venue and paranormal investigation site.

**Buena Vista Cemetery**: Gustave Englebrecht's grave and generations of Port Gamble families.

### Tourism and Events

Port Gamble has embraced its role as a **National Historic Landmark**:

- **Self-guided walking tours** with red plaques marking historic structures
- **Port Gamble Historic Museum** (designed with Smithsonian assistance)
- **Old Mill Days Festival** (July): craft stands, logging show, fireworks
- **Ghost Conference** (annual): paranormal lectures and investigations
- **Ghoulish Gala** (Halloween): masquerade ball

The town is owned by **OPG Port Gamble LLC** (a subsidiary of Rayonier, which acquired Pope Resources in 2020). About 60 miles of trails surround the town for hiking and outdoor recreation.

## Legacy: The Finest Lumber Town on the Pacific Coast

The National Park Service called Port Gamble **"the finest example of a nineteenth-century Pacific Coast logging community."**

It represents:

- **The timber industry** that built the Pacific Northwest
- **Company town paternalism** at its most successful
- **New England architecture** transplanted to Washington
- **142 years of continuous operation**, a record unmatched in American lumber history

Walking the streets of Port Gamble today, you can still hear echoes: the steam whistle calling workers to their shifts, the crash of massive trees hitting the ground, the laughter from the community hall on Saturday nights, the bells of St. Paul's Church on Sunday mornings.

The mill is gone. But Port Gamble endures, frozen in time, a perfect snapshot of the world the lumber barons built.
