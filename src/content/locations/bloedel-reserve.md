# Bloedel Reserve: The Timber Baron's Garden of Redemption

## 1951: A Magnate Retires to an Island

When **Prentice Bloedel** bought 67 acres of logged forest on the north end of Bainbridge Island in 1951, he was one of the wealthiest men in the Pacific Northwest. His father, **Julius Bloedel**, had built **MacMillan Bloedel Ltd.**, one of the world's largest forest-products companies, controlling vast timber holdings across British Columbia and Washington.

For decades, Prentice had run the family business. He oversaw the cutting of old-growth forests, the operation of massive sawmills, the shipping of lumber worldwide. The Bloedel name meant timber, wealth, and power.

But Prentice was different from other timber barons. He called himself a **"reluctant timber baron."** He had wanted to be a teacher. His father convinced him to join the company in 1929, and Prentice spent the next 22 years proving he could build an empire.

In 1951, at age 51, he merged the family company with H.R. MacMillan Export Co., creating MacMillan Bloedel Ltd. Then he did something unusual: he retired.

He moved to Bainbridge Island with his wife **Virginia Merrill Bloedel**, bought 67 acres of cutover land (the property would later expand to roughly 140 acres), and began the work that would define the rest of his life.

## The Paradox: A Timber Baron Planting Trees

The land Prentice bought had been logged. The massive old-growth Douglas firs and western red cedars were gone, cut by companies like the one his family owned. What remained was **second-growth forest**: young trees, scrub brush, and the stumps of giants.

Other timber barons built mansions, collected art, endowed universities. Prentice Bloedel decided to heal the land.

For the next **37 years** (1951-1988), he and Virginia worked with landscape architects, gardeners, and ecologists to transform logged timberland into a garden that honored nature rather than dominated it.

## The Vision: Process Over Perfection

Prentice didn't hire a landscape architect and walk away. He was involved in every decision, every planting, every stone placement.

His philosophy was unusual: **embrace process over rigid master plans**. He believed landscapes should evolve, that "mistakes" could become beautiful, that layers of history should remain visible.

He said: **"Man is not set apart from the rest of nature… we are trustees in this world."**

This was a radical statement for a timber baron.

### The Collaborators

Prentice and Virginia worked with the greatest landscape architects of the era:

**Thomas Church (1954-1976)**: Designed the driveway approach, main pond, and reflection pool. Church was California's preeminent modernist landscape architect.

**Fujitaro Kubota (1955-1961)**: Created the **Japanese Garden**, a pond-style stroll garden on the shore that would later be ranked among the top ten Japanese gardens in America.

**Richard Haag (1978-1985)**: Designed the Moss Garden, Reflection Garden, and Garden of Planes. Haag later won the ASLA Presidential Award for Design Excellence for his work at Bloedel.

**Koichi Kawana, Iain Robertson, and Paul Hayden Kirk** also contributed designs.

## The Japanese Garden: East Meets Northwest

In 1955, Prentice and Virginia commissioned **Fujitaro Kubota** to design a Japanese garden.

Kubota was a Seattle-based landscape architect and nursery owner who had designed gardens across the Pacific Northwest. (His own Kubota Garden in Seattle is now a public park and National Historic Landmark.)

At Bloedel Reserve, Kubota created a **pond-style stroll garden** that blended traditional Japanese design with Pacific Northwest ecology. The garden featured:

- A tranquil pond with koi fish
- A guest house blending Japanese tea house and Pacific Northwest longhouse architecture (designed by Paul Hayden Kirk)
- Carefully placed stones, moss-covered paths, and native plants
- Water features fed by natural groundwater (Prentice insisted on working with the land, not against it)

The **Journal of Japanese Gardening** has twice named Bloedel's Japanese Garden among the **top ten Japanese gardens in the United States**.

## The Moss Garden: 40+ Species of Green

In 1978, Prentice hired **Richard Haag** to design the Moss Garden, a project that would take seven years to complete.

Haag created the **largest moss garden in Kitsap County**, featuring **40+ species of moss** cultivated in a forest clearing. The garden feels ancient, primordial, as if humans stumbled upon a place that existed long before they arrived.

Walking through the Moss Garden, you see dozens of shades of green: emerald, jade, olive, chartreuse. The ground is soft underfoot. Everything is silent except for birdsong and the rustle of wind in the canopy.

The Moss Garden embodies Prentice's philosophy: **slow beauty, patient cultivation, respect for natural processes**.

## The Reflection Pool: Geometry in the Forest

One of the most striking spaces at Bloedel Reserve is the **Reflection Pool**, a rectangular pool framed by **12-foot yew hedges** in a forest clearing.

The design is stark, geometric, almost severe. The pool is perfectly still. The hedges are clipped into sharp lines. The sky reflects on the water.

It's the opposite of the Moss Garden's organic chaos. Here, human order meets wilderness. The contrast is intentional.

Prentice wanted visitors to experience both: the wild and the controlled, the natural and the cultivated, the forest and the garden.

## The Bird Marsh: Enhancing Nature

Prentice didn't just plant gardens. He enhanced natural wetlands to support wildlife.

The **Bird Marsh** features steep drop-offs and nesting islands designed to attract waterfowl and shorebirds. It's not a garden for humans to walk through, it's a habitat for birds, frogs, and insects.

This reflected Prentice's conservation ethic: **humans are part of nature, not separate from it**.

## The Bloedel Philosophy: Sustainable Forestry Ahead of Its Time

Prentice's work at the Reserve mirrored his work in the timber industry.

In **1938**, decades before "sustainable forestry" became mainstream, Prentice launched a **reforestation program**, the first company in the Pacific Northwest to plant seedlings after logging.

By the 1940s, his company was responsible for **70% of reforestation in British Columbia**.

He pioneered waste reduction:
- Reusing "hog fuel" (sawdust and wood chips) to generate power for sawmills
- Inventing early fuel briquettes called **"pres-to-logs"** from sawmill waste

He believed timber companies had a responsibility to replant, restore, and sustain forests for future generations.

This was controversial. Most timber companies practiced "cut and run" logging, clear-cutting forests and moving on. Prentice argued that forests were renewable resources if managed correctly.

His work at Bloedel Reserve was an extension of this philosophy: **cut the forest, then heal it**.

## 1988: Opening to the Public

By the 1970s, maintaining the Reserve had become too expensive for the Bloedels. In **1970**, they donated the property to the **University of Washington**, but the university struggled to afford upkeep.

In **1974**, the Bloedels established **The Arbor Fund**, a nonprofit endowment, to preserve the Reserve.

In **1985**, the Arbor Fund purchased the Reserve from the university.

On **1988**, the **Bloedel Reserve opened to the public** as a 140-acre garden and forest preserve.

Prentice was 88 years old. He had spent 37 years building the garden. Now anyone could visit.

## Prentice Bloedel (1900-1996): The Reluctant Timber Baron

Prentice Bloedel was born in **Bellingham, Washington, in 1900**. His father, Julius Bloedel, had emigrated from Germany and built a timber empire in the Pacific Northwest.

Prentice graduated from **Yale University** and became a teacher. He loved education and had no interest in the family business.

But in **1929**, his father convinced him to join the company. Prentice proved to be a brilliant businessman. He modernized operations, expanded markets, and grew MacMillan Bloedel into one of the world's largest forest-products companies.

But he never stopped thinking of himself as a teacher.

In retirement, he taught through the landscape. The Reserve was his classroom. Every garden, every path, every carefully placed stone was a lesson: **humans can heal what they damage**.

### Art Collectors and Philanthropists

Prentice and Virginia were also major art collectors. They donated **Henri Matisse's *Odalisque*** to the Seattle Art Museum (though it was later discovered to have been looted by the Nazis and was returned to the heirs).

Their daughter, **Virginia Wright**, continued the family's philanthropic tradition, supporting Seattle's art institutions for decades.

Prentice died in **June 1996** at age 95 in his Seattle home, 45 years after buying the land, 8 years after opening it to the public.

## The Reserve Today

Today, the Bloedel Reserve is:

- **150 acres** of gardens and forest (84 acres preserved as second-growth forest)
- A National Historic Landmark for landscape architecture
- Home to the **top-ranked Japanese Garden**, Moss Garden, Reflection Pool, Bird Marsh, and miles of trails
- A center for mindfulness, meditation, creative residencies, and environmental education

**Pay-what-you-wish Wednesdays** ensure the Reserve remains accessible.

**Land acknowledgment**: The Reserve sits on the ancestral territory of the **Suquamish Tribe** (*dxʷsəq'ʷəb*, "place of clear salt water").

## Legacy: Redemption Through Beauty

The Bloedel Reserve is a paradox: a garden built by the wealth of logging, designed to heal the scars of logging.

It represents:

- **Sustainable forestry** pioneered before it was mainstream
- **Asian-Pacific Northwest fusion** in landscape design
- **Process-based design** that embraces evolution and "mistakes"
- **Conservation philanthropy** by a timber baron who believed in restoration

Prentice Bloedel spent the first half of his life cutting trees. He spent the second half planting them.

The Reserve is his apology, and his legacy.
