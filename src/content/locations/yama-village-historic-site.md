# Yama Village: A Community Carved from the Hillside

  The beach below you looks unremarkable today: driftwood, stones, the familiar sounds of Puget Sound. But from 1883 to 1922, this hillside above Port Blakely was home to one of the most vibrant Japanese immigrant communities in the Pacific Northwest. Yama Village, whose name means "mountain" in Japanese, was not just housing. It was a world unto itself.

  ## The Mill and the Men

  The story of Yama Village begins with timber. The Port Blakely Mill, located just down the shore, was one of the largest lumber mills in the world during the late 19th century. It ran around the clock and consumed an enormous amount of labor. Japanese immigrants, many arriving from Hokkaido and Honshu with few possessions but enormous determination, found steady work here.

  The mill company built Yama Village on the hillside to house its Japanese workers, a practical decision that became the foundation of something far more significant. What began as company housing evolved into a genuine community with its own institutions, traditions, and identity.

  ## A Village Complete

  At its peak, over 300 people called Yama home. The village was organized along the hillside in terraces connected by wooden stairs and pathways. Residents built and tended Japanese-style gardens on every available slope, growing vegetables and flowers that reminded them of home.

  The community sustained itself through shared institutions. A general store stocked familiar foods and goods. A community bathhouse provided the social rituals of Japanese bathing culture. A Buddhist temple offered spiritual life and served as a gathering place for festivals and ceremonies. A Japanese-language school ensured that children remained connected to their heritage even as they adapted to American life.

  ## The Asahi Baseball Club

  Perhaps no symbol captures the spirit of Yama Village better than baseball. The community reportedly fielded a team known as the Asahi Baseball Club, said to have competed across the Puget Sound region and to have been a source of enormous local pride. That Japanese immigrants, working long hours in a demanding mill and navigating a foreign culture, would build a baseball diamond and field a competitive team says something profound about human resilience and the drive to belong.

  ## A Cultural Center for the Sound

  Yama Village extended its influence well beyond its borders. Festivals, ceremonies, and celebrations drew Japanese Americans from Seattle, Tacoma, and communities throughout the Puget Sound region. The village functioned as an informal cultural capital, a place where immigrants could speak their language, practice their faith, and reconnect with traditions they had carried across the Pacific.

  ## The End of Yama

  By the early 1920s, the Port Blakely Mill had declined from its peak, and the economic foundation of Yama Village began to erode. As work dried up and the Alien Land Laws made property ownership impossible for Japanese immigrants, residents scattered, with many relocating to the growing Japanese American communities in Winslow and Seattle.

  By 1922, Yama Village was gone. The buildings were removed and the hillside slowly returned to forest. Today, only the most careful observers find evidence of what once stood here: old foundation stones, remnants of garden terraces, cherry trees descended from original plantings, and fragments of pottery in the soil.

  ## The Larger Story

  The residents of Yama Village and their descendants carried the community's legacy forward, into Bainbridge Island's farms and businesses, into the civic life of Seattle, and eventually into the forced exile of World War II internment. Many of the Japanese American families interned at Manzanar and other camps had roots in communities like Yama.

  That history makes this quiet hillside one of the most layered and significant sites on Bainbridge Island: a place where the ambitions, struggles, and resilience of an immigrant community shaped the region in ways that still echo today.
