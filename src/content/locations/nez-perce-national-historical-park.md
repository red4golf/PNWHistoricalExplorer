# Nez Perce National Historical Park: Friendship and Betrayal

Spanning 38 sites across Idaho, Montana, Oregon, and Washington, Nez Perce National Historical Park tells the complex story of the Nimiipuu (Nez Perce) people, from their generous friendship with Lewis and Clark to their tragic forced exodus from their ancestral homeland. This unique multi-state park preserves both the sites where peaceful cooperation flourished and the battlefields where that friendship was shattered by American expansion and broken promises.

## The First Friendship

In September 1805, the Lewis and Clark expedition faced starvation in the Bitterroot Mountains when they encountered the Nez Perce people near present-day Weippe, Idaho. The Corps of Discovery was in desperate condition, exhausted, sick, and nearly out of food after their grueling mountain crossing. The decision by Nez Perce leaders to help rather than harm these strangers would prove pivotal to American history.

Chief Twisted Hair and other Nez Perce leaders provided the expedition with food, horses, and most importantly, canoes and guides for the final journey to the Pacific Ocean. At Canoe Camp along the Clearwater River, Nez Perce men helped the expedition hollow out dugout canoes from giant ponderosa pines, teaching techniques that enabled Lewis and Clark to reach the Columbia River and complete their transcontinental journey.

The relationship deepened during the expedition's return journey in 1806, when the Corps spent a month at Long Camp near present-day Kamiah, Idaho, waiting for snow to clear in the mountain passes. During this extended stay, Captain William Clark provided medical treatment to injured Nez Perce, while the expedition members learned about Nez Perce culture, geography, and traditional ecological knowledge.

## A Partnership of Mutual Respect

The Nez Perce spent more time with Lewis and Clark than any other tribe during the expedition, developing relationships that extended far beyond mere survival assistance. Nez Perce guides led the expedition through complex mountain terrain that would have been impossible to navigate alone. In return, expedition members shared technological knowledge and provided medical care that saved Nez Perce lives.

These interactions established patterns of cooperation that continued for decades. The Nez Perce adopted certain American technologies while maintaining their traditional ways of life. They welcomed missionaries, established peaceful trading relationships, and even sent delegations to Washington, D.C., to negotiate formal agreements with the federal government.

The friendship was so strong that when gold was discovered in Nez Perce territory in 1860, tribal leaders initially allowed miners to work their lands peacefully, believing that American law would protect their treaty rights. This trust would prove tragically misplaced as thousands of miners invaded Nez Perce territory, bringing violence, environmental destruction, and pressure for further land cessions.

## Sacred Homeland

The Nez Perce homeland encompassed approximately 13 million acres across present-day Idaho, Washington, Oregon, and Montana, an area larger than many states. This vast territory included the Blue Mountains, the Wallowa Valley, the Snake River canyon, and portions of the Columbia River plateau, providing diverse ecosystems that supported sophisticated seasonal migration patterns.

The Wallowa Valley in northeastern Oregon held special significance as the heart of traditional Nez Perce culture. Chief Joseph's band, known as the Wallowa Nez Perce, considered this valley their Eden, a place of abundant salmon runs, rich meadows for horse herds, and sacred sites that connected them to their ancestors and spiritual beliefs.

The area surrounding Heart of the Monster near Kamiah, Idaho, serves as the creation site in Nez Perce tradition, where Coyote defeated a monster that was devouring all living beings and created the Nez Perce people from the monster's blood. This sacred geography provided not just physical sustenance but spiritual identity that bound the Nez Perce to their homeland across generations.

## Broken Treaties and Forced Exodus

The 1855 Treaty of Walla Walla initially preserved much of the Nez Perce homeland while establishing peaceful relationships with the United States. However, the discovery of gold and increasing pressure from settlers led to the Treaty of 1863, which reduced the Nez Perce reservation to one-tenth its original size and excluded the beloved Wallowa Valley entirely.

Chief Joseph's father, Old Joseph, refused to sign the 1863 treaty, declaring that he would rather die than sell his homeland. When he died in 1871, he made his son promise never to give up the Wallowa Valley. Young Joseph honored this promise, insisting that the non-treaty Nez Perce had never agreed to leave their ancestral territory.

The crisis reached a breaking point in 1877 when General Oliver Howard gave the non-treaty Nez Perce 30 days to move to the reservation. Rather than submit to forced relocation, approximately 750-800 Nez Perce, including men, women, children, and elders, began an epic 1,170-mile flight toward freedom in Canada.

## The Long Journey

The Nez Perce flight of 1877 became one of the most remarkable military campaigns in American history. Pursued by multiple Army units including the 7th Infantry and 7th Cavalry, the Nez Perce traveled through Idaho, Montana, and Wyoming, fighting 18 engagements while protecting their families and attempting to reach sanctuary with Sitting Bull's Lakota in Canada.

The journey tested every aspect of Nez Perce leadership, military skill, and community solidarity. Chiefs Joseph, Looking Glass, White Bird, and Toohoolhoolzote coordinated strategy while maintaining tribal unity under impossible conditions. Warriors fought brilliant tactical actions that earned respect from Army officers, while women, children, and elders endured hardships that would have broken less cohesive communities.

At White Bird Canyon, Big Hole, Camas Meadows, and other battlefields now preserved within the historical park, visitors can trace the route of this epic journey. Each site tells part of the larger story of indigenous resistance to forced assimilation and the tragic consequences of broken promises and cultural misunderstanding.

## The Surrender

The flight ended on October 5, 1877, in the Bear Paw Mountains of Montana, just 40 miles from the Canadian border. Surrounded by Army forces and facing winter weather with inadequate supplies, Chief Joseph delivered his famous surrender speech: "From where the sun now stands, I will fight no more forever."

The surrender at Bear Paw represented not just military defeat but the end of Nez Perce independence. Despite promises that they would be returned to the Northwest, the survivors were exiled to Kansas and later to Oklahoma, where many died from disease and despair. Chief Joseph spent the rest of his life advocating for his people's return to their homeland, achieving only partial success before his death in 1904.

## Preservation and Remembrance

Today, Nez Perce National Historical Park preserves 38 sites that tell the complete story of Nez Perce history, from ancient cultural traditions through the Lewis and Clark friendship to the tragedy of 1877 and beyond. The park's unique multi-state format reflects the geographic scope of Nez Perce culture and history, requiring visitors to travel the same landscapes that shaped this remarkable people.

At Spalding, Idaho, the park's headquarters museum displays traditional clothing, artifacts, and exhibits that celebrate Nez Perce culture while explaining their complex relationship with American expansion. The Wallowa Band Nez Perce Trail Interpretive Center in Oregon provides access to the homeland that Chief Joseph's people never forgot, while battlefield sites throughout the region honor both the courage of Nez Perce resistance and the tragedy of their defeat.

## Legacy of Friendship and Loss

The Nez Perce story challenges simple narratives about American westward expansion. Their initial friendship with Lewis and Clark demonstrated the potential for mutual respect and cooperation between indigenous peoples and American explorers. Yet their ultimate betrayal and exile revealed the tragic consequences of a national policy that prioritized expansion over justice.

Chief Joseph's surrender speech and his subsequent advocacy for indigenous rights helped establish him as a symbol of noble resistance and dignified leadership in the face of overwhelming adversity. His words continue to resonate today as a reminder that America's westward expansion came at enormous human cost, particularly for Native American peoples who lost not just land but entire ways of life.

The Nez Perce National Historical Park preserves these complex stories not as distant history but as foundational experiences that continue to shape relationships between indigenous peoples and the United States. By visiting sites where friendship flourished and where it was betrayed, visitors can understand both the promise and the tragedy of American expansion while honoring the memory of a people who chose cooperation over conflict until cooperation became impossible.
