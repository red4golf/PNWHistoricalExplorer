# Mount Rainier: The Mountain That Is God

Rising 14,410 feet above sea level, Mount Rainier dominates the skyline of the Pacific Northwest as the most heavily glaciated peak in the lower 48 states. Known to indigenous peoples as "Tahoma" or "Takhoma", meaning "mother of waters", this massive stratovolcano has inspired reverence, challenged climbers, and served as a cornerstone of American conservation for over a century.

## Sacred Mountain of Many Nations

Long before European explorers arrived, Mount Rainier served as the spiritual and physical center of life for multiple Pacific Northwest tribes. The Nisqually, Puyallup, Muckleshoot, Cowlitz, Squaxin Island, Yakama, and Coast Salish peoples have called this region home for thousands of years, with archaeological evidence showing human use dating back over 9,000 years.

More than 100 archaeological sites around the mountain reveal sustained use for hunting and gathering. Native peoples climbed high into the alpine zones to hunt marmots and mountain goats, gather huckleberries and bear grass, and conduct spiritual ceremonies. The high elevations were often seen as the domain of spirits and deities, making the mountain a sacred place where the earthly and divine realms intersected.

The Puyallup name "Tahoma" reflects the mountain's role as the source of life-giving water. Twenty-six major glaciers and 36 square miles of permanent snowfields feed countless rivers and streams that sustain the entire Puget Sound region, justifying the title "mother of waters."

## Birth of a National Park

Mount Rainier became America's fifth national park on March 2, 1899, when President William McKinley signed landmark conservation legislation. The path to protection began in 1893 when the area was designated as the Pacific Forest Reserve, then became Mount Rainier Forest Reserve in 1897.

Key advocates included John Muir, who climbed Mount Rainier in 1888 and wrote passionately about the experience, helping secure park protection. Geologist Bailey Willis started the campaign for national park status in 1893, while forest agent Cyrus Mosier recommended keeping the area "unsullied" for a "great public park."

Congress dedicated the park "for the benefit and enjoyment of the people" and "for the preservation from injury or spoliation of all timber, mineral deposits, natural curiosities, or wonders within said park", establishing principles that would guide American conservation for generations.

## Mountaineering History and Tragedy

Mount Rainier's climbing history began with the first documented ascent on August 17, 1870, by Hazard Stevens and Philemon Beecher Van Trump, guided by Sluiskin, a Yakama leader. Sluiskin was so convinced they would die that he promised to wait two days before reporting their deaths, making their successful return to Olympia's streets a hero's welcome.

Early climbing milestones include the first female ascent by Fay Fuller in 1890, the first winter ascent in 1922, and the legendary first ascent of Liberty Ridge in 1935, now considered the mountain's most iconic and dangerous route.

Today, Mount Rainier attracts thousands of summit attempts annually across more than 20 different climbing routes. Over 439,460 people climbed Mount Rainier between 1950-2018, though the mountain demands respect, requiring technical glacier travel skills, crampons, ice axes, and ropes even on the most popular Disappointment Cleaver Route.

## Glacial Grandeur and Climate Change

Mount Rainier's 26 major glaciers represent the largest glacial system in the lower 48 states, creating some of the most spectacular alpine scenery in North America. The Nisqually Glacier, measured moving 29 inches per day in May 1970, demonstrates the dynamic nature of these frozen rivers.

During the last ice age 25,000-15,000 years ago, glaciers extended all the way to the Puget Sound Basin. Today, these glaciers face new challenges from climate change, with recent decades showing significant glacial retreat and thinning. Stevens Glacier was removed from the inventory in 2021 due to dwindling size, a stark reminder of our changing climate.

The mountain's volcanic nature adds another layer of complexity. As an active stratovolcano with two volcanic craters, Mount Rainier contains the world's largest volcanic glacier cave network within its ice-filled craters and the highest crater lake in North America at 14,203 feet elevation.

## Conservation Challenges in the Modern Era

Mount Rainier National Park faces contemporary challenges that test the balance between preservation and public access. Over 2 million annual visitors create overcrowding pressures, while climate change accelerates glacial retreat and alters alpine ecosystems.

The mountain's volcanic nature presents ongoing concerns, Mount Rainier is considered one of the world's most dangerous volcanoes due to its proximity to populated areas. Lahar risk threatens 80,000 people in surrounding valleys, requiring constant monitoring and emergency preparedness.

Modern management emphasizes collaboration with associated tribes on watershed protection and restoration, military partnerships for prairie habitat restoration, and ongoing efforts to balance recreational access with environmental protection. The Civilian Conservation Corps historically built trails, shelters, and infrastructure that continue to serve millions of visitors today.

## A Mountain for All Time

Mount Rainier represents the intersection of indigenous heritage, conservation history, recreational opportunity, and environmental challenge. From the Paradise visitor center's year-round access to the seasonal Sunrise facility, from Camp Muir's high camps for climbers to extensive trail systems for day hikers, the park serves diverse audiences while protecting irreplaceable natural and cultural resources.

As climate change and increasing visitation pressure test traditional management approaches, Mount Rainier continues to inspire new generations of conservationists, mountaineers, and nature lovers. The mountain that indigenous peoples knew as the "mother of waters" remains a powerful symbol of the American conservation movement and evidence of the enduring value of protecting our most magnificent landscapes for future generations.
