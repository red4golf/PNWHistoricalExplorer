# Lake View Cemetery: Where Seattle Says Goodbye to Bruce and Brandon Lee

The cemetery sits on a quiet hill above Capitol Hill's rooftops, its paths winding through Douglas firs and cedars that have stood here since the 1870s. Lake View Cemetery holds many of Seattle's founding families — pioneers, civic leaders, early settlers whose names appear on street signs and neighborhoods throughout the city. But for the past half-century, most visitors have come looking for a specific pair of polished granite headstones near the eastern edge of the grounds.

## Bruce Lee's Seattle

Bruce Lee arrived in Seattle in 1959, eighteen years old and carrying little more than family connections and an extraordinary restlessness. His father, the Cantonese opera star Lee Hoi-chuen, had sent him from Hong Kong to America partly to create distance from the street trouble he had fallen into as a teenager. Seattle had a small but established Chinese American community, and family connections made it a reasonable landing point.

What nobody could have anticipated was how deeply the city would shape him.

Lee finished his high school diploma at Edison Technical School and worked as a live-in waiter at Ruby Chow's restaurant on Broadway, one of Seattle's first Chinese restaurants to serve a broad clientele. He read voraciously — philosophy, physics, psychology — checking armloads of books out of the Seattle Public Library and filling notebooks with ideas about movement, energy, and human potential. In 1961 he enrolled at the University of Washington, where philosophy became a defining intellectual pursuit. The interest was deliberate. Lee was less concerned with the mechanics of martial arts than with the underlying principles governing all physical movement and performance. His UW notebooks contain passages from Descartes and Krishnamurti alongside diagrams of striking techniques and training schedules.

## The Jun Fan Gung Fu Institute

While still a student, Lee opened his first formal martial arts school on Capitol Hill. The Jun Fan Gung Fu Institute — Jun Fan being his Chinese given name — occupied a small space on University Way and later on East Pine Street. He taught Wing Chun kung fu to whoever wanted to learn, regardless of race or background.

That last point was not trivial. Traditional martial arts schools in 1960s Seattle were largely segregated by custom, with Chinese schools teaching only Chinese students. Lee refused this arrangement. His willingness to teach anyone who showed genuine commitment made him both celebrated and controversial within Seattle's Chinese American community, and it planted the seeds of what would eventually become known as Jeet Kune Do.

## Linda Emery and a Family Rooted Here

At the University of Washington, Lee met Linda Emery, a student from Everett who had become one of his martial arts students. They married in 1964. Their son Brandon was born in Oakland in 1965, after the family moved south following Lee's graduation. But Seattle remained the anchor — Linda's family was here, and the connections Bruce had built during his most formative years gave the city a permanent place in the Lee family's story.

## The Legacy

By the time of Bruce Lee's death in Hong Kong on July 20, 1973, at age 32, he had already altered global culture in ways that are difficult to fully measure. His films had made martial arts cinema into a worldwide phenomenon. His philosophy of combat had challenged the rigid traditions of every established martial arts system. His insistence that physical training was inseparable from philosophical inquiry gave serious intellectual credibility to disciplines that had often been treated as mere athletics.

Linda Lee brought his body home to Seattle. He is buried here, at Lake View Cemetery, beneath a polished granite headstone engraved with his name in both English and Chinese characters.

## Brandon

Brandon Lee grew up partly in his father's enormous shadow and partly, as he got older, in his own emerging light. He had his father's physical gifts and his mother's roots in the Pacific Northwest, and by his early thirties he had built a serious acting career that was finally standing on its own terms.

He was filming The Crow in Wilmington, North Carolina, in March 1993 when a prop accident on set ended his life. He was 28. Linda Lee brought him home as well, and he was buried beside his father.

The matched headstones stand a few feet apart beneath the trees. Brandon's carries a line, drawn from Paul Bowles's novel *The Sheltering Sky*, that has stayed with many visitors: *Because we don't know when we will die, we get to think of life as an inexhaustible well.* It was a passage Brandon had chosen to read at his wedding to fiancée Eliza Hutton — a wedding that was only weeks away when he died.

## A Living Memorial

What distinguishes these graves from most cemetery memorials is the constancy of visitors. Flowers arrive daily, left by people from every country and walk of life. Martial arts students come with offerings. Film enthusiasts come to pay respects. Tourists who have traveled from Japan, Brazil, or Germany include it on itineraries alongside Pike Place Market and the Space Needle.

The offerings left at the headstones are eclectic: flowers, incense, coins, handwritten notes, photographs, sometimes a worn copy of one of Lee's books. Cemetery staff collect and respectfully remove them regularly to make room for the next day's arrivals.

What draws people here is harder to define than simple celebrity. Bruce Lee was the first Asian man to carry American films as a lead, and his impact on representation — on who gets to be the hero of the story — resonates differently for different visitors. For many Asian Americans, the pilgrimage carries a weight that goes beyond admiration for an actor or martial artist.

For others it is simpler: a remarkable man lived his most formative years in this city, studying philosophy at the UW and teaching in a small studio on Capitol Hill, and this quiet hill is where that story has its ending.
