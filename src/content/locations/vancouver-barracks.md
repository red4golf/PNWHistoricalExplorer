# Vancouver Barracks: 162 Years of Service in the Pacific Northwest

## May 13, 1849: The First Flag

On **May 13, 1849**, First Artillery Companies L and M marched up the bluff above the Hudson's Bay Company's Fort Vancouver and raised the American flag over **Camp Vancouver**, the first U.S. Army post in the Pacific Northwest.

Just three years earlier, the **1846 Oregon Treaty** had settled the border with British Canada at the 49th parallel, placing Fort Vancouver under American jurisdiction. The Hudson's Bay Company fur traders still occupied the riverside fort below, but the future belonged to the United States Army.

The post was established in the aftermath of the **1847 Whitman Massacre**, the killing of Presbyterian missionaries Marcus and Narcissa Whitman by Cayuse Indians near present-day Walla Walla. The massacre triggered the Cayuse War and convinced the U.S. government that the Oregon Territory needed a permanent military presence.

The soldiers built their camp **20 feet above** the Hudson's Bay trading post on a strategic bluff overlooking the Columbia River. From here, they could protect American settlers, enforce U.S. sovereignty, and project military power across the vast, unsettled Pacific Northwest.

The post went through four names:
- **Camp Vancouver** (1849-1850)
- **Columbia Barracks** (1850-1853)
- **Fort Vancouver** (1853-1879)
- **Vancouver Barracks** (1879-2011)

For 162 years, the American flag flew over Vancouver Barracks. It would become the longest-serving military post in the Pacific Northwest.

## The Department of the Columbia: Governing a Vast Frontier

Vancouver Barracks became the **headquarters of the Department of the Columbia**, the U.S. Army's administrative command for the entire Pacific Northwest.

The Department encompassed:
- **Oregon Territory** (later Oregon and Washington states)
- **Alaska** (after the 1867 purchase from Russia)
- **Portions of Idaho**

This was an immense area, hundreds of thousands of square miles of wilderness, mountains, forests, and rivers. The Army's mission: protect settlers, build infrastructure, enforce treaties, and suppress Native American resistance.

Vancouver Barracks became the nerve center for all military operations in the region.

## September 20, 1852: Captain Ulysses S. Grant Arrives

In September 1852, a 30-year-old **Brevet Captain named Ulysses S. Grant** arrived at Columbia Barracks (as it was then called) with the **4th Infantry Regiment**.

Grant had graduated from West Point in 1843, served in the Mexican-American War (1846-1848), and earned a reputation as a competent but unremarkable officer. He was assigned to Columbia Barracks as the **regimental quartermaster**, responsible for managing supplies, logistics, and provisions.

### Grant's 15 Months in the Pacific Northwest

Grant lived in the **"Quartermaster's Ranch"**, a prefabricated two-story New England home shipped around Cape Horn and assembled near the Columbia River waterfront. (He did not live in the "Grant House" on Officers Row, which was actually the company commander's headquarters.)

His duties included:
- Managing the **Quartermaster's Depot** (storage and distribution of military supplies)
- Overseeing construction of military roads
- Running **potato farms** to supplement his income (a common practice for underpaid officers)

Grant struggled with the isolation of frontier life. His wife Julia and their young son Frederick were back east, he couldn't afford to bring them west on his meager Army salary. A second son was born around the time he left for the Pacific Northwest, a child Grant had not yet met. He tried business ventures to earn extra money, including shipping ice from the Columbia River to San Francisco. All failed.

He spent long hours riding his horse along the Columbia River to Portland, exploring the wilderness, and drinking to cope with loneliness.

A persistent local story holds that during a hard freeze Grant walked across the frozen Columbia River, though the claim is unverified.

In early 1854, Grant was transferred to **Fort Humboldt, California**. Within months, he resigned from the Army, citing financial pressures and separation from his family. For the next seven years, he struggled as a farmer and clerk, drifting toward obscurity.

Then the **Civil War** began in 1861.

By 1865, **General Ulysses S. Grant** had led the Union Army to victory, saved the United States, and become one of the most famous men in the world. He served two terms as President (1869-1877).

The quartermaster who managed potato farms at Columbia Barracks became the man who won the Civil War.

### The Grant House Today

The **Grant House** (built 1849) is the **oldest building at Vancouver Barracks**. It's named in Grant's honor, though he never actually lived there.

Today it houses the **Eatery at Grant House**, a restaurant where visitors can dine in the same building where officers planned military campaigns in the 1850s.

## 1936-1938: General George C. Marshall Commands the Post

In 1936, **Brigadier General George C. Marshall** was assigned to command Vancouver Barracks and the **5th Infantry Brigade**.

Marshall was already a rising star in the Army, respected for his strategic brilliance, organizational skills, and calm leadership. At Vancouver, he commanded troops and oversaw the **9th Corps District of the Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC)**, managing 55+ camps across the Pacific Northwest during the Great Depression.

Marshall lived in the **Marshall House** (built 1886), a Queen Anne Victorian on Officers Row.

Two years later, in 1938, Marshall was promoted and transferred. He would go on to:
- Serve as **U.S. Army Chief of Staff** during World War II
- Author the **Marshall Plan** for European reconstruction (1947)
- Serve as **Secretary of State** (1947-1949) and **Secretary of Defense** (1950-1951)
- Win the **Nobel Peace Prize** (1953), the only career soldier ever to receive it

The Marshall House stands today as evidence of the general who lived there before reshaping the post-war world.

## The Pacific Northwest Indian Wars (1848-1879)

For 30 years, Vancouver Barracks served as the command center for **U.S. Army operations against Native American tribes** resisting American expansion.

The Army fought more than a dozen conflicts:

- **Cayuse War** (1847-1855)
- **Rogue River Wars** (1855-1856)
- **Puget Sound War** (1855-1856)
- **Yakima War** (1855-1858)
- **Coeur d'Alene War** (1858)
- **Snake War** (1864-1868)
- **Modoc War** (1872-1873)
- **Nez Perce War** (1877)
- **Bannock War** (1878)
- **Sheepeater Indian War** (1879)

Vancouver Barracks was the **staging ground, supply depot, and headquarters** for all these campaigns. Soldiers marched from the parade grounds to battlefields across Oregon, Washington, and Idaho. Supplies were shipped from the Quartermaster's Depot to remote outposts. Wounded soldiers returned for medical treatment.

The post also served as a **prison for Native American leaders and prisoners of war**.

### Over 70 Future Generals Served Here

Vancouver Barracks became a proving ground for Army officers. Dozens who served here later became generals during the Civil War and beyond:

**Union Generals**:
- **George B. McClellan** (commander of the Army of the Potomac)
- **Philip H. Sheridan** (cavalry commander)
- **Oliver O. Howard** (Medal of Honor recipient, founder of Howard University)
- **Benjamin Alvord** (military governor)

**Confederate Generals**:
- **George Pickett** (Pickett's Charge at Gettysburg)
- **William Wing Loring**
- **Gabriel J. Rains**

In total, approximately **70 officers who served at Vancouver Barracks attained the rank of general**.

## World War I: The Spruce Production Division (1917-1918)

When the United States entered World War I in 1917, the Army needed **massive quantities of spruce lumber** to build airplanes. Spruce was lightweight, strong, and flexible, perfect for aircraft frames.

The Pacific Northwest had the best spruce forests in the world. But labor strikes, low wages, and dangerous conditions in logging camps threatened to cripple production.

The Army created the **Spruce Production Division** under **Colonel Brice Disque**, headquartered at Vancouver Barracks.

### The World's Largest Spruce Mill

In 1917, the Army built the **world's largest spruce cut-up mill** at Vancouver Barracks. The facility employed thousands of soldiers and civilians to process spruce into aircraft-quality lumber.

At its peak:
- **30,000 soldiers** were assigned to Vancouver Barracks
- The post expanded to **3,019 acres**
- Facilities could house **250 officers and 7,295 enlisted personnel**

The Spruce Production Division provided **most of the spruce lumber for Allied airplanes** during World War I.

After the war, the mill was razed, and the post returned to peacetime operations.

## World War II: Staging Area for the Pacific Theater (1941-1945)

When Japan attacked Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, Vancouver Barracks became a **staging area for the Seattle Port of Embarkation**.

Over **30,000 soldiers** passed through Vancouver Barracks on their way to the Pacific Theater. The post trained troops, provided medical services, and coordinated logistics for the war effort.

Vancouver Barracks also housed:
- **Prisoner-of-war camps** for German and Italian POWs (some of whom are buried in the post cemetery)
- **Administrative offices** for regional military coordination
- **Training facilities** for specialized units

The post operated at full capacity throughout the war.

## Post-War Transition: Army Reserve and National Guard (1946-2011)

After World War II, Vancouver Barracks transitioned to an **Army Reserve and National Guard training center**.

The post became home to the **104th Division (Reserves)**, the longest-tenanting unit. Reservists trained here during:
- The **Korean War** (1950-1953)
- The **Vietnam War** (1965-1975)
- The **Cold War**
- The **Global War on Terrorism** (2001-present)

In **2000**, the last active-duty officers departed.

On **September 2011**, Vancouver Barracks officially closed under the **Base Realignment and Closure (BRAC) Commission**.

After **162 years of continuous military service**, the American flag was lowered for the final time.

## Vancouver Barracks National Cemetery: Honoring 175 Years of Service

The **Vancouver Barracks National Cemetery** was established in **1849**, the same year the post was founded.

The first interment occurred on **October 22, 1849**.

Today, the **5.74-acre cemetery** holds **1,500+ graves**, including:

- U.S. military personnel (active duty, veterans, and family members)
- Civilians who served the post
- Foreign military personnel
- **German and Italian POWs** from World War II
- **Native Americans**
- **Unknowns** (over 200 unmarked graves, names lost to history)

### Four Medal of Honor Recipients

Four **Medal of Honor recipients** are buried at Vancouver Barracks National Cemetery:

1. **Sergeant James Madison Hill**
2. **Major William McCammon**
3. **Private Herman Pfisterer**
4. **First Sergeant Moses Williams** (Buffalo Soldier)

### The Buffalo Soldiers

The cemetery holds the graves of **Buffalo Soldiers**, the pioneering Black troops who served in the U.S. Army's segregated regiments during the Indian Wars and Spanish-American War.

The term "Buffalo Soldiers" was coined by Native Americans who respected the soldiers' fierce fighting spirit and dark, curly hair that resembled buffalo manes.

First Sergeant Moses Williams, a Medal of Honor recipient, is among the Buffalo Soldiers buried here.

### Military Funerals and Honors

Since **March 6, 2020**, the cemetery has been managed by the **VA National Cemetery Administration** (previously managed by the U.S. Army).

The cemetery accepts **cremated remains only** (no casketed burials).

Eligible for burial:
- Veterans with honorable discharge
- Active-duty service members
- Spouses and eligible dependents

**Military funeral honors** are provided for all eligible veterans, including:
- Folding and presentation of the U.S. flag
- Playing of "Taps"
- Honor guard (when available)

Families can schedule burials by calling **1-800-535-1117**.

### A Place of Remembrance

Walking through Vancouver Barracks National Cemetery, you see graves spanning 175 years:

- Soldiers who fought in the Indian Wars
- Veterans of the Spanish-American War and Philippine Insurrection
- World War I doughboys
- World War II heroes
- Korean War veterans
- Vietnam War veterans
- Gulf War veterans
- Global War on Terrorism veterans

The cemetery is a living connection to every conflict the United States has fought since 1849.

## Officers Row: 21 Victorian Homes Frozen in Time

**Officers Row** is a tree-lined boulevard featuring **21 restored Victorian homes** (built 1849-1906) that once housed U.S. Army officers stationed at Vancouver Barracks.

The homes represent some of the finest Victorian architecture in the Pacific Northwest:

### The Grant House (1849)

The **oldest building** at Vancouver Barracks. Built from logs and plank siding, it served as headquarters for company commanders. Named in honor of Ulysses S. Grant, though he never lived there.

Today: **Eatery at Grant House** restaurant.

### The Marshall House (1886)

A **Queen Anne Victorian** built for $10,000 (equivalent to $255,000 today). General George C. Marshall lived here from 1936-1938.

Today: Headquarters for **The Historic Trust** and event venue.

### The Howard House (1878)

An **Italianate-style home** where General Oliver O. Howard lived. Howard was a **Medal of Honor recipient** and founder of **Howard University**.

Today: Visitor center and historical exhibits.

### Restoration and Preservation

By the 1970s, Officers Row buildings were deteriorating. The Army declared them surplus, and demolition seemed inevitable.

A **grassroots preservation campaign** saved them. In 1980, the buildings were deeded to the **City of Vancouver for $1**.

Between 1980-1987, a **$10.9 million rehabilitation** restored the homes to their Victorian-era appearance.

Today, Officers Row features:
- **34 residential rental units** (people live in these historic homes)
- **Commercial offices**
- **Event venues** for weddings and gatherings
- **Public walking tours** (self-guided and docent-led)

## Pearson Airfield: One of America's Oldest Airfields

In **1925**, the Army established **Pearson Airfield** at Vancouver Barracks, one of the **oldest continuously operating airfields in the United States**.

The airfield became a center for **Army Air Corps aviation** in the Pacific Northwest. Today, the **Pearson Air Museum** (part of Fort Vancouver National Historic Site) shows aviation history alongside the military post.

## Fort Vancouver National Historic Site: Preserving 175 Years of History

In **1948**, portions of Vancouver Barracks were transferred to the **National Park Service** to establish **Fort Vancouver National Historic Site**.

The site includes:
- **Hudson's Bay Company Fort Vancouver** (reconstructed; original burned 1866)
- **Vancouver Barracks** (parade grounds, barracks buildings, Officers Row)
- **Pearson Airfield and Aviation Museum**
- **Visitor Center** with exhibits on military and fur trade history

In **1996**, Congress established the **Vancouver National Historic Reserve**, a 366-acre area managed by the National Park Service.

## The Legacy of Service

Vancouver Barracks represents **162 years of continuous military service**, longer than any other post in the Pacific Northwest.

It was the headquarters for:
- The Department of the Columbia (1849-1913)
- Pacific Northwest Indian Wars (1848-1879)
- World War I Spruce Production Division (1917-1918)
- World War II mobilization (1941-1945)
- Cold War readiness (1946-1991)
- Global War on Terrorism support (2001-2011)

The post hosted:
- **Ulysses S. Grant**, who became the general who saved the Union
- **George C. Marshall**, who rebuilt Europe after World War II
- **Over 70 future generals** who shaped American military history

Today, Vancouver Barracks is:
- A **National Historic Site** preserving 175 years of Pacific Northwest history
- A **National Cemetery** honoring 1,500+ service members
- A **living community** where people reside in restored Victorian officers' homes
- A place where **military funerals continue to honor** those who served

The soldiers who raised the flag on May 13, 1849, could never have imagined that their remote frontier post would endure for 162 years, host two of America's greatest generals, and become a National Historic Site preserving the memory of every veteran who served here.

But that is the legacy of Vancouver Barracks: **service, sacrifice, and remembrance**.
