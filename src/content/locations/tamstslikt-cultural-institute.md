# Tamástslikt Cultural Institute: Voices of the Plateau Peoples

On the high plateau of northeastern Oregon, the Tamástslikt Cultural Institute shows the enduring presence and rich cultural heritage of the Confederated Tribes of the Umatilla Indian Reservation. This world-class museum, whose name means "interpret" in the Walla Walla language, presents 10,000 years of Cayuse, Umatilla, and Walla Walla history from indigenous perspectives, creating one of the most comprehensive and culturally authentic Native American interpretive centers in the Pacific Northwest.

## The Plateau Peoples: Ancient Inhabitants

For millennia before European contact, the Cayuse, Umatilla, and Walla Walla peoples thrived on the Columbia River Plateau, developing sophisticated societies adapted to the region's diverse ecosystems. These communities created complex seasonal rounds that took advantage of different resources throughout the year, salmon runs in the Columbia River system, camas and other root gathering in mountain meadows, and hunting opportunities in forests and grasslands.

The plateau peoples developed a rich material culture perfectly adapted to their environment. They created distinctive basketry, carved implements from local stone and wood, and developed architectural forms that could be easily transported as communities moved through their seasonal territories. Their sophisticated understanding of ecological relationships sustained large populations while maintaining environmental balance.

Spiritual traditions centered on relationships with the natural world, with ceremonies, oral traditions, and artistic expressions that reflected deep connections to specific places and seasonal cycles. These cultural practices provided both practical knowledge for survival and spiritual guidance for maintaining harmony between human communities and the forces that sustained all life.

## Cultural Confluence and Trade Networks

The Columbia River Plateau served as a major crossroads for trade networks that extended from the Pacific Coast to the Great Plains and from California to Alaska. The annual trade gatherings at Celilo Falls brought together peoples from throughout the West, creating opportunities for cultural exchange, intermarriage, and the sharing of technological innovations that enriched all participating communities.

The Cayuse, Umatilla, and Walla Walla peoples played central roles in these trade networks, serving as intermediaries between coastal peoples and inland communities. Their strategic location along major travel routes and their diplomatic skills made them essential partners for traders and travelers crossing the plateau region.

The introduction of horses in the early 18th century revolutionized plateau life, enabling greater mobility and transforming hunting, trading, and warfare patterns. The plateau peoples became renowned horse breeders and traders. The distinctive Appaloosa breed that remains associated with the region today is historically tied to the neighboring Nez Perce. This equestrian culture connected them to Great Plains traditions while maintaining their distinctive plateau identities.

## Treaty Era and Cultural Survival

The Treaty of 1855 between the United States and the Confederated Tribes fundamentally altered traditional life patterns while establishing the legal framework for the modern reservation. The treaty negotiations, dramatically recreated in the institute's Treaty Council exhibit, involved complex discussions about land cessions, reserved rights, and the relationship between tribal sovereignty and American expansion.

Despite enormous pressures from American settlement, military conflicts, and federal assimilation policies, the confederated tribes maintained essential elements of their cultural identity. Traditional languages, ceremonies, and ecological knowledge continued in modified forms even during periods when federal policies actively discouraged indigenous cultural expression.

The establishment of boarding schools designed to "civilize" Native American children represented one of the most challenging threats to cultural continuity. However, many families found ways to preserve traditional knowledge and values while adapting to changing circumstances, creating the cultural resilience that would prove essential for later revitalization efforts.

## Museum Development and Tribal Self-Determination

Tamástslikt Cultural Institute emerged from the self-determination movements of the late 20th century, when Native American communities began asserting greater control over how their histories and cultures were presented to both tribal members and the general public. The Confederated Tribes of the Umatilla Indian Reservation recognized that a tribally controlled museum could serve multiple purposes, preserving traditional knowledge, educating young people about their heritage, and presenting accurate representations of plateau culture to non-Native visitors.

The institute's development required extensive collaboration between tribal elders, cultural specialists, and museum professionals to ensure that exhibits reflected authentic tribal perspectives rather than external interpretations of indigenous culture. This approach emphasized community involvement in all aspects of museum planning, from selecting artifacts and designing exhibits to developing educational programs and special events.

The museum's name, Tamástslikt, reflects this commitment to authentic interpretation. The word means "interpret" in the Walla Walla language, emphasizing the institute's role as a place where tribal peoples speak for themselves about their history, culture, and contemporary life.

## Immersive Exhibits and Cultural Interpretation

The institute's permanent exhibits use innovative multimedia technology, authentic artifacts, and immersive environments to tell the story of plateau peoples from time immemorial to the present. The "We Were, We Are, We Will Be" exhibit traces 10,000 years of continuous occupation while emphasizing the persistence and adaptation of tribal cultures through dramatic historical changes.

The Seasonal Round exhibit recreates the traditional annual cycle of the plateau peoples, complete with ambient sounds, seasonal lighting, and interactive displays that help visitors understand the sophisticated ecological knowledge that sustained indigenous communities. Visitors experience the sights, sounds, and rhythms of traditional life while learning about the continuing importance of seasonal activities in contemporary tribal culture.

The Treaty Council recreation represents one of the museum's most powerful interpretive experiences. This life-sized diorama places visitors in the midst of the 1855 treaty negotiations, complete with historical figures, period costumes, and multilingual interpretation that reflects the complex linguistic and cultural dynamics of these crucial meetings.

## Contemporary Cultural Preservation

Beyond its interpretive functions, Tamástslikt serves as an active center for cultural preservation and revitalization programs that serve the confederated tribes and the broader community. Language classes, traditional arts workshops, and cultural seminars help community members maintain connections to ancestral knowledge while adapting traditional practices to contemporary circumstances.

The institute's research collections and archives preserve thousands of artifacts, photographs, documents, and oral histories that provide essential resources for tribal members researching their family histories and cultural traditions. These materials also support academic research and educational programs that contribute to broader understanding of plateau culture and history.

Elder programs bring traditional knowledge holders together with younger generations for structured knowledge transfer activities that ensure cultural traditions continue to be transmitted through direct personal relationships. These programs often feature instruction in traditional crafts, foods, and ceremonies that might otherwise be lost to historical disruption.

## Educational Outreach and Public Engagement

Tamástslikt's educational programs serve both tribal and non-tribal audiences through school visits, teacher training workshops, and community events that promote cross-cultural understanding and appreciation for indigenous heritage. These programs emphasize accurate historical information while challenging stereotypes and misconceptions about Native American cultures.

The institute's location near Pendleton makes it accessible to visitors from throughout the Pacific Northwest while serving as a cultural anchor for the reservation community. Special events, including traditional celebrations, powwows, and cultural demonstrations, provide opportunities for visitors to experience living indigenous culture rather than just historical artifacts.

Research partnerships with universities and other museums expand the institute's impact while ensuring that tribal perspectives are included in broader scholarly discussions about plateau history and culture. These collaborations demonstrate how indigenous communities can participate in academic research while maintaining control over how their heritage is studied and interpreted.

Tamástslikt Cultural Institute stands as a powerful example of tribal self-determination and cultural sovereignty, demonstrating how indigenous communities can preserve their heritage while educating the broader public about the rich cultures that continue to thrive in the Pacific Northwest.
