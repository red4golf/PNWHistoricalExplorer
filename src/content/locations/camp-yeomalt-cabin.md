## WPA and the New Deal in the Pacific Northwest

The Camp Yeomalt Cabin represents one of the most transformative periods in American history, Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal programs designed to combat the Great Depression. The Works Progress Administration (WPA) put millions of unemployed Americans to work on public projects across the nation, creating lasting infrastructure while restoring dignity to workers who had lost everything in the economic collapse.

In the Pacific Northwest, the WPA built everything from post offices to parks, from highways to forest trails. These projects left an indelible mark on the region's landscape and demonstrated the federal government's commitment to conservation and public recreation. The Camp Yeomalt Cabin stands as a tangible reminder of this era, built in 1935 by unemployed Bainbridge Island residents who transformed local Douglas-fir into a magnificent log lodge using traditional craftsmanship techniques.

## Depression-Era Craftsmanship

When the WPA hired local men for the Camp Yeomalt project, they weren't just offering paychecks, they were offering purpose. These men, many of them former fishermen, loggers, and farmers, brought real skills to the work. They felled massive old-growth Douglas-fir from the surrounding forests and shaped each log with expert precision.

The result was Camp Major Hopkins, named after Major Maurice Jayne Hopkins. The cabin featured no nails or screws in its original construction, just expertly notched logs fitted together using traditional techniques that dated back centuries. The crowning achievement: a dramatic stone chimney with fireplaces on both interior and exterior sides, creating gathering spaces that would warm generations of island youth.

## Boy Scout Movement

The cabin was built specifically to serve the Boy Scouts of America, an organization that played a crucial role in American youth development during the early 20th century. Scouting emphasized outdoor skills, character building, and civic responsibility, values that resonated deeply during the uncertain years of the Depression and World War II.

Camp Hopkins became a place where Bainbridge Island boys learned to tie knots, identify trees, cook over open fires, and develop leadership skills. The camp represented the democratization of outdoor recreation, making wilderness experiences accessible to youth regardless of their families' economic circumstances, a revolutionary concept for the era.

## World War II and Japanese Internment

In March 1942, the peaceful Boy Scout camp witnessed one of the darkest chapters in American history. Bainbridge Island became the first community in the entire United States subjected to Executive Order 9066, forcing all residents of Japanese ancestry to leave their homes with just six days' notice and one suitcase each.

The US Army 202nd Coast Artillery commandeered Camp Hopkins as their Bainbridge Island headquarters. Soldiers drilled where scouts had camped. The building that was meant to bring joy to children now served a military watching for threats along the Pacific Coast. A bathhouse was hastily constructed during this period, where the Camp Yeomalt Classroom now stands.

This transformation of the cabin from youth camp to military headquarters symbolized the dramatic changes war brought to American communities, particularly in the Pacific Northwest where fear of Japanese attack ran highest.

## Post-War Scouting Years

After the war ended in 1945, Camp Hopkins returned to its original purpose. For more than four decades, the cabin served generations of Bainbridge Island scouts. The logs echoed with campfire songs, the Scout Oath, and the laughter of boys learning outdoor skills that would stay with them for life.

During this period, scouting experienced its golden age in America. Veterans returning from war valued the character-building aspects of the program, and enrollment surged. Camp Hopkins became a cherished part of island life, connecting young people to both outdoor skills and community tradition.

## Community Ownership and Preservation Crisis

In 1987, the Bainbridge Island Park and Recreation District acquired the property from the Camp Hopkins Youth Committee and Boy Scouts, ensuring public access for future generations. By 2000, the camp was renamed Camp Yeomalt Park, honoring its deep connection to the community.

But by 2005, the cabin faced its greatest threat. Rot and insect infestation from decades of Pacific Northwest rain had taken a devastating toll. The Park District seriously considered demolition, viewing the deteriorating structure as a liability rather than an asset worth saving.

## The Power of Community Preservation

Preservationist Jerry Elfendahl refused to accept the cabin's demolition. He launched an extraordinary public campaign that rallied the community around their shared heritage. Team Yeomalt formed, volunteers with expertise in preservation, log construction, and local history who understood that this wasn't just a building, but a tangible connection to multiple layers of American history.

In 2005, the cabin was listed on the National Register of Historic Places, validating its historical significance. Over the next five years, the community raised over $280,000 from multiple sources: the Bainbridge Island Park District, Bainbridge Island Park Foundation, City of Bainbridge Island, state preservation grants, and hundreds of individual donors.

Thousands of volunteer hours poured into the restoration. Creasey Log Homes was contracted for the delicate log renovation work. Rotten logs were painstakingly removed, the building jacked up, new footings and concrete walls poured, all while preserving original materials and Depression-era construction methods to maintain historical integrity.

## Recognition and Rebirth

On August 28, 2010, the community gathered for the grand reopening and rededication ceremony. Every restored log testified to community determination and respect for history. The cabin stood proud once again, a monument to the idea that some things are worth saving.

In 2011, Team Yeomalt and Bainbridge Island Parks received the Washington State Historic Preservation Award in the Stewardship Category, recognizing not just the technical excellence of the restoration but the community spirit that made it possible. The award highlighted the cabin as a model for community-driven historic preservation across the state.

## Contemporary Significance

Today, the 1,200-square-foot cabin accommodates up to 50 people and is available for public rental through the Bainbridge Island Metro Park & Recreation District. Visitors still gather around that magnificent stone fireplace beneath the open beam ceiling. The small performance stage hosts events, while the outdoor patio fireplace evokes memories of countless campfires past.

The Camp Yeomalt Cabin connects us across time to the desperate Depression-era workers who built it, the scouts who grew up here, the soldiers stationed here during wartime, and the preservationists who refused to let it die. It stands as a living monument to multiple layers of Pacific Northwest history: the New Deal's promise of work and dignity, the complexities of World War II on the home front, the scouting movement that shaped young lives, and the power of community preservation.

As you visit this cabin on its 3-acre woodland property with trails and historic structures, you're walking through nearly a century of Pacific Northwest history. The logs that Depression-era workers so carefully placed still stand, ready to welcome another generation to this remarkable place.
