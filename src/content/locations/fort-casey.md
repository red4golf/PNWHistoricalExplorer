# Fort Casey: Sentinel of Admiralty Inlet

  Stand at the edge of the gun battery and look out across Admiralty Inlet. On a clear day you can see the Olympic Peninsula to the west and the Cascade foothills to the east. What you are seeing is nearly the same view soldiers scanned anxiously for enemy warships from the 1890s through the 1940s, a century of vigilance from this windswept bluff on Whidbey Island.

  ## The Triangle of Fire

  Fort Casey was one point in a carefully calculated military strategy known as the Triangle of Fire. Military planners in the 1890s recognized that any hostile fleet threatening Puget Sound would have to pass through Admiralty Inlet, a narrow, fast-moving channel that offered a defender's dream of concentrated firepower.

  Three forts were constructed to cover this passage from different angles: Casey on Whidbey Island, Worden on the Olympic Peninsula, and Flagler on Marrowstone Island. Their overlapping fields of fire would theoretically make the inlet impassable to any enemy vessel. It was sound thinking for its era, though the strategy would eventually be rendered obsolete by the emergence of air power.

  ## The Disappearing Guns

  The centerpiece of Fort Casey's armament was its battery of disappearing guns, a technology that was thoroughly practical military engineering. Each massive gun was mounted on a hydraulic counterweight carriage. When not firing, the barrel sat completely hidden below the concrete parapet, invisible to enemy rangefinders. To fire, the crew would raise the gun, take aim, pull the lanyard, and the recoil would automatically return the barrel to its hidden position below the wall.

  The guns you see at Fort Casey today (two 10-inch pieces) are not the originals. The original armament was removed during World War I and II when the metal was desperately needed for the war effort. These guns were retrieved from Fort Wint in the Philippines after World War II and installed here in the 1960s for historical display.

  At peak armament, Fort Casey could fire 600-pound projectiles up to eight miles into the inlet, enough range to engage a warship before it could return accurate fire at the shore batteries.

  ## Inside the Batteries

  Descend into the underground magazines and you enter a remarkably intact world. The thick concrete walls, designed to absorb direct artillery hits, muffle the wind and create a dim, cool silence. Soldiers worked in carefully coordinated teams here: one crew loading and ramming powder charges, another operating the mechanical fire control equipment, calculating range and bearing from reports relayed by observation stations positioned along the coast.

  The plotting rooms contained early mechanical computers, intricate brass and steel instruments that translated observer reports into firing solutions. Every round required constant recalculation as a moving target changed course and speed. It was exacting, demanding work performed under the stress of potential combat.

  ## Admiralty Head Lighthouse

  The lighthouse standing at Fort Casey is the second structure built on this location. The original Admiralty Head Lighthouse was built in 1861 and guided ships through the inlet for decades. When the fort was constructed, the military demolished the original light and built the current structure in 1903 to serve both as a navigation aid and as a fire control observation post.

  The lighthouse was decommissioned in 1922 when a lightship anchored in the shipping lane made it redundant (the lantern room was later removed in 1927). For decades it stood empty. Today, restored to its Victorian appearance, you can climb the spiral staircase to the lantern room and look out across the same waters that keepers watched through all weather for more than sixty years.

  ## World War II and After

  By World War II, the Triangle of Fire concept had been recognized as inadequate against modern naval threats, and the big guns were largely ceremonial deterrents. Fort Casey served primarily as a training facility during the war, preparing infantry soldiers before they shipped out to the Pacific and European theaters.

  After the war ended, the military's need for coastal fortifications evaporated. Fort Casey was decommissioned and transferred to Washington State in 1956, becoming one of the most distinctive state parks in the region: a place where you can walk the same ground soldiers walked, peer into the dark magazines where powder charges were stored, and stand at the parapet where men once scanned these waters watching for threats that, thankfully, never came.

## Admiralty Head Lighthouse: Guardian of the Triangle of Fire

### 1890s: The Spanish-American War Changes Everything

In the 1890s, America's military strategists faced a problem: the Pacific Coast was virtually undefended. If a hostile fleet entered Puget Sound, it could shell Seattle, Tacoma, Bremerton, and Everett without resistance.

The **Spanish-American War (1898)** heightened these fears. What if Spain, or another naval power, attacked the West Coast?

The U.S. Army decided to fortify the entrance to **Admiralty Inlet**, the narrow passage connecting the Strait of Juan de Fuca to Puget Sound. Control Admiralty Inlet, and you control access to the entire Puget Sound basin.

The solution: the **"Triangle of Fire"**, three coastal defense forts positioned to create overlapping fields of artillery fire that would "rain death on any enemy vessels attempting to enter Admiralty Inlet."

### The Triangle of Fire: Three Forts, One Mission

In the late 1890s, the Army built three forts:

1. **Fort Worden** (Port Townsend)
2. **Fort Flagler** (Marrowstone Island)
3. **Fort Casey** (Whidbey Island)

Each fort was armed with massive **disappearing guns**, artillery pieces mounted on special carriages that raised them from protective emplacements just long enough to fire, then lowered them back down for reloading and protection from enemy counter-battery fire.

**Fort Casey** was the southernmost point of the triangle, positioned on Whidbey Island's western shore.

#### Fort Casey: The Arsenal

Construction began in **1897**. The fort was named after **Brigadier General Thomas Lincoln Casey**, U.S. Army Chief of Engineers.

By the time Fort Casey was activated in **1901**, it bristled with **34-35 artillery pieces**:

- **6 × 10-inch disappearing guns** (firing 617-pound shells up to 8 miles)
- **6 × 6-inch disappearing guns**
- **16 × 12-inch mortars** (lobbing shells in high arcs to hit ship decks)
- Various 3-inch and 5-inch pedestal guns for close defense

The first test firing occurred on **May 5, 1903**. The roar of the guns echoed across Admiralty Inlet, announcing that Puget Sound was now defended.

### The Problem: Where to Put the Lighthouse

There was a problem.

Since 1861, the **Red Bluff Lighthouse**, a wooden structure, had guided ships into Admiralty Inlet from the eastern shore. But when the Army planned Fort Casey's gun emplacements, they discovered the lighthouse sat exactly where they needed to install a **10-inch gun battery**.

The lighthouse had to move.

But simply relocating the old wooden structure wasn't enough. The new lighthouse needed to:

1. **Withstand concussions** from the fort's massive guns firing just yards away
2. **Serve as a navigational aid** for steamships entering Puget Sound
3. **House the lighthouse keeper and family** in a structure safe from artillery blasts

### 1903: Building Admiralty Head Lighthouse

In **1903**, the Army constructed a new lighthouse on **Admiralty Head**, a bluff on Fort Casey's grounds.

The design was striking: **Spanish-style architecture** with brick covered in stucco, designed by architect **Carl Leick**.

#### The Structure

**Height**: 30 feet tall  
**Elevation**: 127 feet above sea level (positioned on a bluff)  
**Design**: Two-story dwelling connected to a circular tower with a one-story foyer  
**Lens**: Fourth-order Fresnel lens (imported from France)  
**Modern amenities**: Indoor bathroom and laundry room, luxuries in 1903

The lighthouse was built **sturdy enough to withstand the shock waves** from Fort Casey's 10-inch disappearing guns firing nearby.

The lighthouse was **activated on June 25, 1903**.

### Life at Admiralty Head: Keepers and Families

Lighthouse keepers and their families lived in the attached dwelling. They maintained the lens, lit the lamp at sunset, extinguished it at dawn, and kept meticulous logs of weather, shipping traffic, and lighthouse operations.

The keeper's job was isolation mixed with responsibility. Admiralty Head was remote, accessible by boat or a long walk from Coupeville. Families were largely self-sufficient, growing vegetables, fishing, and ordering supplies from Seattle.

Children attended school in Coupeville when weather permitted. Wives managed households in the shadow of artillery batteries.

When the fort's guns fired during training exercises, the lighthouse shook. The concussions rattled windows and sent vibrations through the floors. But the structure held.

### Steamships and the Golden Age of Puget Sound Navigation

From 1903 to the early 1920s, Admiralty Head Lighthouse guided **steamships** into Puget Sound.

These were the great passenger and cargo steamers of the early 20th century:

- **Pacific Coast Steamship Company** vessels running between Seattle and San Francisco
- **Puget Sound mosquito fleet** ferries connecting small towns
- **Canadian Pacific Railway steamers** from Victoria and Vancouver
- **U.S. Navy vessels** heading to Bremerton Naval Shipyard

Steamship captains relied on lighthouses to navigate at night and in fog. Admiralty Head's fourth-order Fresnel lens could be seen for miles, marking the entrance to Admiralty Inlet.

### 1922: Decommissioned After Just 19 Years

On **July 1, 1922**, Admiralty Head Lighthouse was **decommissioned**, after only **19 years of service**.

Why?

**Steam-powered vessels** no longer needed the eastern-side navigation aid. Ships could navigate more precisely with steam power, and most captains preferred to hug the **western shore of Admiralty Inlet**, away from the lighthouse.

The lighthouse became redundant.

The lens was removed. The structure was abandoned. For decades, it sat empty within Fort Casey, slowly deteriorating.

### Fort Casey Through Two World Wars

While the lighthouse was decommissioned, Fort Casey remained active.

#### World War I (1917-1918)

Fort Casey served as a coastal defense installation, though no enemy vessels ever approached Puget Sound. Soldiers drilled, maintained guns, and prepared for an attack that never came.

#### Interwar Period (1919-1941)

The fort was placed on caretaker status. A skeleton crew maintained the guns and facilities, but Fort Casey was largely obsolete. Naval aviation and submarines had made coastal artillery less relevant.

#### World War II (1941-1945)

After Pearl Harbor, Fort Casey was briefly reactivated. The Army feared Japanese attacks on the West Coast. But by 1943, it was clear the Japanese lacked the capability to strike Puget Sound.

The fort was **decommissioned permanently in 1953**, ending over 50 years of coastal defense.

### Restoration: Bringing the Lighthouse Back to Life

In the 1950s, **Island County Historical Society** and **Washington State Parks** began efforts to restore Admiralty Head Lighthouse.

#### 1957: Major Restoration Begins

Volunteers and preservationists stabilized the structure, repaired the stucco exterior, and restored the interior.

#### 1962: A New Lens

A **fourth-order Fresnel lens** from **Alki Point Lighthouse** in Seattle was installed for display. (The original lens had been removed in 1922.)

#### 1973: National Register of Historic Places

Admiralty Head Lighthouse was added to the **National Register of Historic Places**, recognizing its historical and architectural significance.

#### 2010-2012: Rebuilding the Lantern Room

The most ambitious restoration came between 2010 and 2012, when students from **three Whidbey Island high schools** (Oak Harbor, Coupeville, South Whidbey) rebuilt the **lantern house** with help from **Nichols Brothers Boat Builders**.

In **August 2012**, a crane lifted the new **5,000-pound authentic lantern room** into place atop the lighthouse tower.

### Fort Casey State Park: Exploring the Triangle of Fire

Today, Admiralty Head Lighthouse stands within **Fort Casey Historical State Park**, a 467-acre park featuring:

- **The lighthouse** (open seasonally with interpretive center, historical museum, and gift shop)
- **Gun batteries** with restored artillery emplacements and tunnels
- **Keystone Underwater Dive Park** (one of the best cold-water dive sites in Washington)
- **Beaches, hiking trails, and camping**
- **Pacific Northwest Trail access** (long-distance hiking trail)

#### Visiting the Lighthouse

**Hours**: Noon-4pm (seasonal schedule varies, March to November)  
**Admission**: Free with Washington State Discover Pass or $10 day-use fee  
**Tours**: Self-guided; interpretive displays explain coastal defense history

From the lighthouse, visitors can see across Admiralty Inlet to **Fort Worden** (Port Townsend) and glimpse the defensive triangle that once protected Puget Sound.

### The Legacy of Coastal Defense

Admiralty Head Lighthouse and Fort Casey represent a brief but crucial era in American military history: **coastal artillery defense** (1890s-1940s).

For 50 years, the U.S. Army believed massive shore-based guns were the best defense against enemy fleets. Forts like Casey, Worden, and Flagler were built at tremendous expense across the country.

But technology changed. By World War II, **aircraft carriers, submarines, and long-range bombers** made coastal artillery obsolete. No enemy fleet ever challenged the Triangle of Fire. The guns never fired in anger.

Yet the forts served their purpose: they deterred attack simply by existing.

#### Why the Lighthouse Matters

Admiralty Head Lighthouse is more than a navigational aid. It's a symbol of:

- **The steam age of navigation** on Puget Sound
- **Military engineering** designed to survive artillery concussions
- **Community preservation** by Island County volunteers and Washington State Parks
- **The Triangle of Fire** coastal defense system

Today, standing in the lantern room 127 feet above Admiralty Inlet, you can see what lighthouse keepers saw 120 years ago: ships passing through the strait, the Olympic Mountains to the south, the San Juan Islands to the north.

The guns are silent. The steamships are gone. But the lighthouse endures, a guardian watching over the waters it once lit.

## Admiralty Head Lighthouse: Beacon of Puget Sound Navigation

Standing proudly at the entrance to Admiralty Inlet on Whidbey Island, Admiralty Head Lighthouse represents both the golden age of lighthouse construction and the strategic importance of Puget Sound navigation during the early 20th century. Built in 1903 as part of the comprehensive coastal defense system that included Fort Casey, this elegant Italianate Revival structure served mariners for two decades while witnessing the transformation of the Pacific Northwest from frontier territory to modern naval power.

### Strategic Location and Construction

The Lighthouse Board selected Admiralty Head for its commanding position overlooking the entrance to Puget Sound, where the busy shipping lanes required reliable navigation aids to prevent disasters in the treacherous waters. The site's 127-foot elevation above sea level provided optimal visibility for the lighthouse's beacon, which could be seen for 17 nautical miles on clear nights.

Construction began in 1902 using the finest materials and craftsmanship available. The lighthouse's distinctive Italianate Revival architecture, featuring red brick walls, white trim, and classical proportions, reflected the Lighthouse Board's commitment to creating structures that combined functional excellence with architectural dignity. The building's substantial construction, with walls nearly two feet thick, was designed to withstand the severe winter storms that regularly battered the Pacific Northwest coast.

The lighthouse originally featured a fourth-order Fresnel lens manufactured in Paris, France, by the renowned Barbier, Benard & Turenne company. This sophisticated optical system, consisting of hundreds of precisely cut glass prisms, focused the lamp's light into a powerful beam that could penetrate fog and guide vessels safely through Admiralty Inlet's hazardous passages.

### Fort Casey Integration

Admiralty Head Lighthouse was constructed as an integral component of Fort Casey, one of three coastal defense installations built to protect the Puget Sound Naval Shipyard at Bremerton. The lighthouse served dual military and civilian functions, guiding commercial vessels and naval ships while supporting the fort's coastal defense mission during an era when foreign naval attack remained a genuine concern.

The lighthouse's location within the fort complex required careful coordination between civilian lighthouse keepers and military personnel. The keeper's residence was built adjacent to the tower, creating a small civilian enclave within the military reservation. This unique arrangement reflected the importance both military and civilian authorities placed on maintaining reliable navigation aids for Puget Sound's increasingly busy maritime traffic.

During World War I, the lighthouse took on additional strategic importance as naval activity in Puget Sound intensified dramatically. The beacon helped guide naval vessels, merchant ships carrying war supplies, and passenger steamers through waters that had become crucial to the Allied war effort. The lighthouse keeper's detailed logbooks from this period document the dramatic increase in maritime traffic and the challenges of maintaining navigation services during wartime conditions.

### Lighthouse Keeper Life

The lighthouse keepers who staffed Admiralty Head faced unique challenges that combined the traditional duties of lighthouse keeping with the complexities of operating within a military installation. Head keeper quarters provided comfortable accommodations for families, but the isolated location and demanding schedule of lighthouse maintenance required dedication and self-reliance that characterized lighthouse keeping throughout the Pacific Northwest.

Daily duties included maintaining the Fresnel lens, monitoring weather conditions, recording maritime traffic, and ensuring the lighthouse's mechanical systems operated reliably. The keeper climbed the tower's spiral staircase multiple times daily to tend the light, clean the lens, and check for approaching vessels that might require assistance. During storms, keepers remained on constant duty to ensure the light remained operational when mariners most desperately needed guidance.

The lighthouse keeper's family became part of the broader Fort Casey community, with children attending the military post's school and participating in social activities that brought together civilian and military families. This integration created unique childhood experiences for lighthouse keepers' children, who grew up surrounded by both maritime and military traditions.

### Technological Evolution and Decommissioning

The rapid advancement of navigation technology during the early 20th century gradually reduced the lighthouse's operational importance. Radio beacons, improved charts, and electronic navigation aids provided mariners with more reliable guidance than traditional lighthouses could offer. Additionally, changes in shipping patterns as larger vessels increasingly used deeper-water channels reduced traffic through Admiralty Inlet.

In 1922, the Lighthouse Service decommissioned Admiralty Head Lighthouse after only 20 years of operation. The relatively brief service life reflected the rapid technological changes that were transforming maritime navigation throughout the world. The Fresnel lens was removed and relocated to other lighthouse stations where it continued serving mariners for many more years.

The lighthouse's decommissioning marked the end of an era when lighthouses represented the most advanced navigation technology available. However, the building's solid construction and architectural merit ensured its survival as military housing and later as a historical monument that would interpret lighthouse heritage for future generations.

### Maritime Museum Development

Following the lighthouse's decommissioning, the building served various military functions until Fort Casey's closure after World War II. When Washington State Parks acquired the fort property, preservationists recognized the lighthouse's historical significance and potential for interpreting Pacific Northwest maritime heritage.

The transformation of Admiralty Head Lighthouse into a maritime museum required extensive restoration to return the building to its original appearance while adapting interior spaces for exhibit purposes. The lighthouse tower was restored with period-appropriate fixtures, while the keeper's quarters were converted into gallery spaces that could house maritime artifacts, historical photographs, and interpretive displays.

The museum's exhibits focus on lighthouse technology, Puget Sound navigation history, and the daily lives of lighthouse keeping families. Visitors can climb to the lighthouse tower's lantern room for spectacular views of Admiralty Inlet while learning about the sophisticated optical systems that made lighthouse beacons visible for miles across dark waters.

### Preservation and Public Access

Today, Admiralty Head Lighthouse serves as both a beloved historical landmark and an active educational facility that welcomes thousands of visitors annually. The lighthouse's location within Fort Casey State Park provides easy public access while protecting the structure within a preserved historical landscape that includes the original fort installations.

The lighthouse's museum functions are supported by volunteer docents who share their knowledge of lighthouse history and maritime heritage with visitors from around the world. Educational programs bring school groups to the lighthouse for hands-on learning experiences about navigation technology, coastal geography, and the maritime industries that shaped Pacific Northwest development.

Ongoing preservation efforts ensure that Admiralty Head Lighthouse will continue serving educational purposes for future generations while maintaining its architectural integrity and historical authenticity. The lighthouse stands as a monument to the skill and dedication of the lighthouse keepers who served mariners during the golden age of Puget Sound shipping.
